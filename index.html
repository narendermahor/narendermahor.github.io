Job market paper Automation Labor economics

Labor Unrest and the Automation Response: Evidence from High-Severity Workplace Incidents

Narender Mahor and John Ampong

We examine whether firms accelerate automation adoption following high-severity labor incidents. Using a novel dataset that links RepRisk labor-incident records to earnings-call transcripts for U.S. public firms over 2007-2023, we employ a difference-in-differences framework augmented with Callaway and Sant'Anna event studies to identify the causal effect of workplace disruptions on firms' automation strategies.

Baseline estimates indicate that firms experiencing a high-severity labor incident increase automation-related discussion in earnings calls by approximately 5 percentage points, a roughly 27% increase relative to the sample mean. The effect is concentrated among services-sector firms.

Working paper Labor economics

Does Unionization Shield Routine Workers from Automation? Evidence from the China Import Shock

This paper examines whether unionization mitigated the negative employment effects of Chinese import competition for routine and non-routine workers. The analysis uses Current Population Survey data and UN Comtrade bilateral trade data with an instrumental variables design.

Findings indicate larger employment losses in Right-to-Work states, where unions are weaker, with effects concentrated among women in routine jobs. Earnings effects are statistically insignificant.

Working paper India Crime Female Employment

Does Crime Affect Women's Labor Participation in Traditional Jobs? Evidence from India

This study evaluates how crime affects women's labor participation in India using IHDS and NCRB data. The empirical strategy uses exogenous variation from pending high-court cases in an instrumental variables framework.

The estimates suggest that increases in total IPC crimes and crimes against women reduce participation in traditional jobs and lower annual hours worked.
